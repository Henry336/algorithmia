# Algorithmia Chronological Story Overview

Last updated: 2026-07-11

This file is just the story in chronological order.

Not the design goals. Not the arcade mode. Not the implementation notes. Just what happened, what is happening, and what probably happens later, based on the lore we have agreed on so far.

Some names are still placeholders, especially the exact name of the world's main power source. But the actual direction is here.

Inspirations to keep in mind:

- Null is inspired by Death from *Puss in Boots: The Last Wish*. Calm, inevitable, personal, frightening, and not just "evil because evil."
- Unbounded is inspired by Baal / the Raiden Shogun from *Genshin Impact*. A ruler obsessed with stability, eternity, and stopping progress because change feels like the path back to disaster.
- The overall gameplay feel is inspired by *Deltarune*: RPG exploration, strange enemies, personality-heavy battles, humor, unsettling story turns, and combat that feels alive instead of being just menus or test cases.

## 1. Before Everything Went Wrong

Algorithmia used to be a world where code was not just something people wrote on a screen.

Code was basically a dialect of reality.

Different programming languages were like different dialects that could interact with the world's systems. For now, the protagonist's dialect is Python, but other dialects can exist later.

The world itself ran on structures, routines, contracts, memory, paths, priorities, and rules. Data structures and algorithms were not just school topics. They were how things lived, moved, remembered, organized themselves, and repaired themselves.

Somewhere at the center of all of this was a major power source.

We still need the final name, but it might be something like the Root Engine, Runtime Heart, Prime Compiler, Core Archive, or Source Kernel.

Whatever we call it, it was not just a battery.

It was tied to identity, memory, life, dialects, growth, and change. It was the thing that let beings remain themselves while still being able to evolve. It was basically one of the deepest reasons Algorithmia could exist as a living world.

But civilization eventually started abusing it.

People became too good at patching things, extending things, preserving things, reviving things, duplicating things, and refusing to let anything end.

At first this probably looked amazing.

People could prevent loss. They could extend life. They could bring back corrupted files. They could repair broken systems. They could keep people alive longer. They could optimize suffering away.

But the problem is that not every ending is a bug.

Some systems needed to stop.

Some memories needed to rest.

Some lives had reached their natural limit.

Some corrupted things could not be safely restored without becoming something else.

And the world kept refusing to accept that.

## 2. What Null Was Supposed To Be

Null was not originally evil.

Null was Death.

Not death as a random monster. Not death as just "the bad guy." Null was the natural force of endings.

Null ended failed processes. Null cleared memory that could not safely be restored. Null released beings from corrupted suffering. Null prevented infinite loops of pain. Null made room for new futures.

In a healthy world, Null was necessary.

Null was not supposed to hate life.

Null was supposed to protect life from becoming endless suffering.

But then the world started treating death as an error. Every ending was resisted. Every deletion was undone. Every released soul was recompiled. Every broken thing was forced to continue because someone believed there had to be one more patch.

So Null watched the world turn continuation into cruelty.

Eventually, Null's purpose twisted.

He came to believe that if continuation caused suffering, then the greatest mercy was to end everything.

That is where he overstepped.

He stopped being the guardian of endings and became the judge of existence.

## 3. The First Timeline Protagonist

In the first timeline, the protagonist was not memoryless yet.

He was powerful, brilliant, and deeply determined to protect his civilization.

He knew dialects. He understood repair. He was probably one of the most talented people alive when it came to using code to stabilize the world.

But his biggest flaw was already there:

he believed that if something broke, he had to be the one to fix it.

If someone suffered, he took it as his failure.

If the world was unstable, he felt personally responsible for making it stable.

This is important because this is not evil at first.

It is actually heroic at first.

But the same instinct that makes someone protective can become dangerous if they start thinking no one else is allowed to fail, choose, risk, change, or end.

The protagonist's core wound was basically:

"If I am not useful enough, people die."

That idea is what eventually becomes Unbounded.

## 4. Null Attacks The Citadel

At some point, Null attacks the Citadel that guards the power source.

The reason is not that Null wants power in a normal villain way.

Null believes the world has become a machine that forces suffering to continue forever. He sees the power source as the thing enabling that. If he corrupts it, or ends it, then maybe the world can finally stop.

The protagonist stands in the way.

He is trying to protect the Citadel, the power source, and everyone connected to it.

But Null overpowers him.

During this battle, Null corrupts the power source. Maybe not completely, and maybe not in a fully controlled way, but enough to damage the deepest layer of the world.

Because the power source is connected to life, identity, memory, and selfhood, the corruption spreads through beings themselves.

Files decay.

Memories rot.

Bodies and minds lose structure.

People begin turning into Null-corrupted versions of themselves.

This is the beginning of Null Rot.

## 5. The Protagonist Hunts Null

The protagonist survives the Citadel disaster, but barely.

He is wounded, corrupted, furious, and probably terrified.

He does not see Null as a necessary force anymore. He sees Null as the thing that ruined everything.

So he hunts Null.

He follows him to the edges of the world.

This part still needs more detail, but the important part is that the protagonist becomes more and more obsessed. His original desire to protect starts turning into something harsher.

He does not just want to stop Null.

He wants to make sure nothing like Null can ever threaten the world again.

That difference matters.

## 6. The First Final Battle At The Citadel

Eventually, Null returns to the Citadel.

The cleanest reason right now is that the first corruption was incomplete. Null's first attack damaged the power source, but did not fully end the world's continuation. So now he comes back to finish it properly.

The protagonist confronts him there.

At this point, the protagonist is already close to corruption and death. But he still throws himself into the disaster.

Null tries to absorb or fully corrupt the power source.

The protagonist intervenes.

Something goes horribly wrong.

The protagonist absorbs part of the power source and part of the corruption. Null and the power source are thrown completely out of balance.

The protagonist becomes unimaginably powerful.

And then he kills Null.

This seems like victory for a moment.

But it is actually one of the worst mistakes ever made.

Because Null was not just an enemy.

Null was Death.

Null was part of the world's balance.

So killing him does not remove endings safely. It makes his nullifying power explode uncontrolled into the deepest parts of reality.

Null Rot spreads even further.

The protagonist saved the world from Null in the most literal way possible, and by doing that, he made the world worse.

## 7. The Birth Of Unbounded

After killing Null, the protagonist becomes known as Unbounded.

He has absorbed part of the power source. He has absurd power. He is also partly corrupted, traumatized, and terrified of ever being helpless again.

This is where he becomes a kind of dictator, but not a simple "I want power" dictator.

He becomes a ruler who genuinely believes control is protection.

His logic is basically:

"Freedom created instability."

"Instability created suffering."

"Suffering created Null."

"So I will remove instability."

He does not just control people directly. He freezes progress.

He believes any advancement might create something that can overthrow him, destabilize the world, or create another Null-level disaster.

Even though he is already too powerful, he is still afraid.

So he bans dangerous research.

He controls dialect development.

He seals algorithms.

He edits history.

He treats experimentation as a corruption risk.

He forces people into stable roles.

He turns "safety" into a cage.

This is the real tragedy of Unbounded:

he is trying to protect the world from collapse, but he protects it by stopping it from living.

## 8. How Unbounded Corrupts The World Further

Unbounded also starts giving beings too much power.

His reasoning is understandable, but still wrong.

He thinks if people had been stronger, Null would not have destroyed them. He thinks if everyone had more power, no one would ever be helpless again.

But power without stable identity does not make people free.

It mutates them.

It exaggerates them.

It turns their worst logic into their whole being.

This is how beings like Bogolord and other corrupted algorithmic tyrants can rise.

They are not just random monsters. They are partly consequences of Unbounded's attempts to empower the world without understanding what that power would do to people.

Sorting becomes judgment.

Priority becomes domination.

Randomness becomes madness.

Optimization becomes cruelty.

Repair becomes ownership.

Unbounded realizes too late that he has not saved civilization.

He has created a frozen, terrified world where everyone is either corrupted by Null Rot, corrupted by excessive power, or trapped under his control.

The people he loved are not really themselves anymore.

And the worst part is that he did all of it because he was trying to protect them.

## 9. Negative Big-O And The Memory Wipe

Eventually, Unbounded tries one last desperate solution.

He tries to gain negative Big-O time complexity.

Basically, he tries to reverse time itself.

He wants to go back and fix everything before the first mistake becomes permanent.

Somehow, it works.

But not cleanly.

The time reversal wipes memory.

More specifically, it wipes space complexity.

The protagonist loses his stored past, but not his innate skill.

His memories are gone.

His talent remains.

That is why the game begins with the protagonist waking up with no memory but strong Python/dialect knowledge.

He does not remember the first timeline.

But his instincts are still shaped by it.

He does not have memories.

He has execution scars.

## 10. The Second Timeline Begins

This is where the player starts.

The protagonist wakes up with no memory of who he is.

But he can still use Python as a repair dialect.

At first, he believes the problem is simple enough:

Null is evil.

Null Rot is spreading.

People need protecting.

He is one of the few people who can repair things with code.

So he starts helping.

He fixes systems. He fights corrupted enemies. He learns how the world works. He probably meets allies like Mira and others who ground him and keep him from becoming too abstract or obsessed.

But there should always be something slightly wrong about him.

He learns too quickly.

Old systems respond to him strangely.

Some enemies recognize something they should not recognize.

He sometimes makes choices like someone trying to avoid a mistake he cannot remember.

When stressed, he may sound uncomfortably close to Unbounded.

The player should not know the full truth yet, but the story should quietly keep asking:

why does this person already know how to do this?

## 11. The Protagonist's Inner Conflict In The Second Timeline

The protagonist is not just fighting enemies.

He is also fighting the same flaw that created Unbounded.

He wants to fix everything.

He wants to be useful.

He wants to prevent pain before it happens.

And because he is talented, people will probably start relying on him. That makes the problem worse.

He can begin to believe that if he is smart enough, fast enough, or strong enough, he can keep everyone safe.

That sounds noble.

But it is the beginning of control.

His arc in the second timeline is learning that he cannot repair the world by owning every outcome.

He has to let allies choose.

He has to let systems have safeguards instead of absolute control.

He has to accept that some losses cannot be optimized away.

He has to understand that endings are not always failures.

This is what lets him eventually become different from Unbounded.

## 12. The 65 Percent Reveal

Around 65% through the full story, the protagonist should discover the truth.

He was Unbounded.

Or more accurately, Unbounded was what he became in the first timeline.

He was the protector who killed Null.

He was the one who absorbed part of the power source.

He was the one who froze progress.

He was the one who gave too much power to beings who could not hold it.

He was the one who tried to reverse time.

The protagonist did not simply wake up as a random hero.

He woke up as the result of a failed attempt to undo his own future.

This reveal should not just be a lore twist.

It should hit his inner struggle directly.

Because now the player understands:

the danger was not only outside him.

The danger was always something he could become.

## 13. The Second Citadel Standoff

Eventually, the second timeline reaches the Citadel again.

But this time, the protagonist makes different choices.

He does not want to kill Null.

He understands, or at least suspects, that killing Null is not the answer.

He tries to stop Null without repeating the first timeline's mistake.

This deviation matters.

Null notices.

Null sees that the protagonist is acting strangely.

Not just emotionally strangely. Causally strangely.

The protagonist has no memory, but he moves like someone who has already seen the battle.

He prepares for things that have not happened.

He avoids choices from the first timeline.

He refuses chances to kill Null.

His dialect skill is too advanced for the life he supposedly lived.

His very being has rewrite scars from the negative-time attempt.

And Null, being Death, can sense things that should have ended.

The protagonist is an undeleted remainder from a timeline that should be gone.

To Null, this is horrifying.

The power source is dangerous, yes.

But the protagonist is a bigger anomaly.

Because if the protagonist can retry reality once, then maybe he can keep retrying until death loses forever.

So Null changes his plan.

In the first timeline, he tried to absorb or corrupt the power source.

In the second timeline, he absorbs the protagonist.

His logic is basically:

"The engine is not the anomaly. You are."

Then Null smashes the power source.

He takes the anomaly and destroys the stabilizer.

That is when reality breaks in a new way.

## 14. Unbounded Null

When Null absorbs the protagonist, he also touches what is buried inside him.

Not just the current protagonist.

The residue of Unbounded.

The fear.

The control.

The corrupted power.

The future self who never learned to stop.

This fusion creates Unbounded Null.

Unbounded Null is pure evil, but it makes sense why.

It is the worst parts of Null and Unbounded fused together.

Null's corrupted belief:

"Existence should end."

Unbounded's corrupted belief:

"Existence must be controlled."

Together, they become something like:

"Everything will be saved by having no will."

"Everything will be preserved by being erased."

This is not mercy anymore.

This is not protection anymore.

This is domination through deletion.

Unbounded Null creates a much worse version of Null Rot. The world enters a nightmare state. Monsters become stronger. Old places become wrong. The rules of battles can break. The game itself can start feeling more hostile, because reality is now running on a fused contradiction.

## 15. The Final Conflict

The final problem cannot be solved by becoming stronger than Unbounded Null.

That would just repeat the original mistake.

The protagonist already tried power.

Power became Unbounded.

So the final answer has to be limits.

The protagonist has to use bounded repair, separation, constraints, memory, and acceptance.

The goal is not simply "kill the final boss."

The goal is to separate what should never have fused:

- Null from Unbounded
- Death from annihilation
- protection from control
- repair from ownership
- power from fear

The protagonist has to prove he has become different from the first timeline version of himself.

He cannot deny Death.

He cannot control all progress.

He cannot save everyone by becoming infinite.

He has to choose a bounded solution.

## 16. Null's Change

Once Null is separated from Unbounded Null, he finally understands what happened to him.

Inside Unbounded Null, Null was not acting as rightful Death.

He was being used as a weapon.

His purpose had become domination by absence.

He sees that pure erasure without mercy is not peace.

It is cruelty.

This is why Null can change without it feeling cheap.

He does not become soft.

He becomes rightful again.

He remembers that he was never supposed to hate existence.

He was supposed to guard endings when endings were truly needed.

The protagonist can finally say, in some form:

"You were never meant to hate life. You were meant to protect it from endless pain."

And Null can understand that the protagonist is not trying to abolish death like Unbounded did.

The protagonist is accepting that life is only real if endings are allowed to exist.

## 17. What Happens To Unbounded

After Null is separated, Unbounded remains.

But he cannot really be fixed.

He is too corrupted. Too entangled with the broken power source. Too full of stolen power, fear, and the old timeline's mistakes.

This is where the story probably needs self-sacrifice or release.

Unbounded may ask the protagonist to kill him.

Not because he suddenly becomes perfectly good.

Because he finally accepts the thing he never could accept before:

some things cannot be repaired.

Some things must be released.

That is also Null's true lesson.

The protagonist ending Unbounded would not be revenge.

It would be mercy and responsibility.

It would be the protagonist refusing to become him.

## 18. Possible Endings

We have not locked the exact ending yet.

The current options are:

### Ending A: The Protagonist Lives

The protagonist kills/releases Unbounded and survives.

He loses the path to infinite power. He remains finite. He keeps memory and responsibility, but he does not become a god.

This is probably the cleanest ending if we want future stories.

### Ending B: The Protagonist Dies Too

The protagonist and Unbounded are too causally linked.

Killing Unbounded deletes the loop, so the protagonist sacrifices himself to let the world continue.

This is tragic and powerful, but maybe too final.

### Ending C: The Protagonist Becomes The New Bound

The protagonist survives, but becomes tied to the Citadel or power source as a guardian of limits.

Not a ruler.

Not a god.

Not another Unbounded.

More like a living constraint that prevents any one being from owning the source again.

This might be the best mythic compromise.

## 19. The Emotional Ending

Whatever exact ending we choose, the emotional ending should be clear.

Null learns that death is not judgment.

Unbounded learns that protection without trust becomes tyranny.

The protagonist learns that repair without limits becomes control.

The world learns that progress has to be allowed, even if it creates risk.

And the protagonist finally stops measuring his worth by whether he can fix everything.

Possible final exchange:

Null:

"You would permit endings?"

Protagonist:

"I would permit life to be real."

That is basically the heart of the story right now.
