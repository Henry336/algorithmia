# Algorithmia Story So Far

Last updated: 2026-07-11

## Purpose Of This File

This is the current story direction for **Algorithmia**.

Important spelling note: the project is called **Algorithmia**, not Algorithimia. Any older file, asset, route, branch, or text using "Algorithimia" is legacy naming and should be corrected over time.

This file is not meant to sound like some polished corporate lore bible. It is a working canon document. Some things are locked enough to build around. Some things are still "probably this, but we should keep thinking." That is fine. The point is to stop losing the story every time context refreshes.

## Current High-Level Direction

Algorithmia is a browser-based programming RPG where code is not just a test-case answer. Code is a dialect that can repair, redirect, stabilize, weaken, reveal, or reshape parts of the world.

The protagonist wakes up with no memory of his past, but somehow still has strong knowledge of a programming dialect. For now that dialect is Python. Later, other languages can exist as other dialects.

The world is not just "fantasy with coding pasted on." Data structures and algorithms are supposed to be the physics, magic, infrastructure, politics, and moral language of the world.

The newer design direction is that battles should have actual battle mechanics. The code should not be the whole fight. Code should give the player an advantage in an unfair fight:

- reveal the true enemy
- slow attacks
- reshape the battlefield
- stabilize tiles
- collapse clones
- repair corrupted routes
- expose weak points
- reduce damage
- convert chaos into something usable

So the player is not just submitting code. The player is using code to survive and exploit enemy mechanics.

## Big Story Thesis

The story is about repair, control, death, progress, memory, and limits.

The protagonist's eventual lesson is not "get stronger until you can fix everything."

The lesson is closer to:

You cannot save the world by controlling every outcome.

You cannot treat every ending as a bug.

You cannot freeze the future just because change might hurt.

You cannot repair people by owning their choices.

This matters because the protagonist's future self already made these mistakes once.

## The Protagonist

The protagonist wakes up with no memory, but his instincts are weirdly advanced.

He does not remember who he was, what happened, or why the world feels familiar. But he understands code. He understands patterns. He can look at broken systems and somehow know how they might be repaired.

That should feel strange even early on.

He is not "chosen" in the normal sense. He is someone who has already failed once and does not remember it.

### His Inner Struggle

The protagonist needs a real internal conflict, not just "am I secretly the villain?"

His struggle is that he ties his worth to being useful.

If something is broken, he feels responsible for fixing it. If someone gets hurt, he takes it personally. If a repair fails, he does not just think "the system failed." He thinks "I failed."

At first this looks heroic. He helps people. He repairs things. He takes responsibility.

But it can turn ugly:

- he over-optimizes small problems because uncertainty scares him
- he gets uncomfortable when allies make risky choices
- he treats incomplete repairs as personal failure
- he starts thinking that if he had just been smarter or faster, nobody would suffer
- he becomes tempted to remove uncertainty instead of living with it

This is the seed of Unbounded.

Real-life parallel: people who build their whole identity around being productive, talented, useful, protective, or "the one who fixes things." It can be good. It can also become control, guilt, burnout, and fear.

The protagonist's arc should be learning that care is not control.

Repairing someone is not the same as owning their outcome.

## Dialects And Repair

Programming languages are dialects.

Python is the protagonist's current dialect because the game is Python-first for now.

A dialect can interact with broken reality because reality in Algorithmia is made of executable customs, old contracts, data structures, and living systems. The right code can inspect, patch, stabilize, reorder, traverse, or constrain those systems.

But code is not automatically good.

Bad code can create brittle repairs.

Overpowered code can erase the thing it tries to save.

Optimized code can still be morally wrong.

This is important. Algorithmia should not teach "code solves everything." It should teach that code is power, and power needs limits, tests, humility, and consequences.

## The Power Source

We still need the exact name, but the current concept is that the world has some central power source that makes beings executable and alive.

Old story used "the Archive." That may still exist, but maybe as only one layer of the larger system.

Possible names:

- the Root Engine
- the Runtime Heart
- the Prime Compiler
- the Core Archive
- the Source Kernel

My current favorite direction is that the power source is not just energy. It is the thing that lets beings have identity, memory, growth, dialect, and change.

This makes it more important than a magic battery.

If it is corrupted, people do not just become sick. Their files, memories, bodies, and selfhood start breaking.

If it is overloaded, people can gain too much power without the identity to hold it safely.

This is where Null Rot comes from.

## Null

Null should represent Death.

Not cartoon death. Not "evil skeleton king" death. More like an ancient force of endings.

Originally, Null was necessary.

Null ended failed processes. Null cleared memory that could not safely be restored. Null released beings from corrupted suffering. Null prevented infinite loops of pain. Null made room for new life, new code, and new futures.

In a healthy world, Null was not evil.

Null was mercy.

### Why Null Oversteps

The world starts abusing the power source.

People stop accepting endings. They resurrect corrupted files. They duplicate minds. They extend lives past stable limits. They patch pain without healing it. They force broken systems and broken beings to keep running because "we can still fix this."

To Null, this is horrifying.

Every deletion is undone.

Every released soul is recompiled.

Every ending is treated as a defect.

Eventually Null changes his thesis:

"If continuation is the source of suffering, then mercy means ending everything."

That is the overstep.

Null stops being the guardian of endings and becomes the judge of existence.

Real-life parallel: death itself is not evil, but fear of death can twist people. Also, despair can make someone believe nonexistence is mercy. Null is that idea turned into a cosmic force.

### Null's Personality

Null should not feel petty.

He should feel calm, inevitable, severe, ancient, and almost compassionate in a frightening way.

He does not rage like a normal villain. He believes he is doing the cleanest possible mercy.

That is what makes him scary.

## First Timeline

This is the hidden tragedy that happened before the game begins.

The protagonist was once a brilliant protector of civilization. He fought to defend the Citadel that guarded the power source.

Null attacked because he believed the world had become a machine for endless suffering.

The protagonist tried to protect everyone.

The fight went badly.

Null overpowered him while he was defending the Citadel and corrupted the power source.

Because the power source was tied to the life force and identity of all beings, that corruption started spreading through the world. Files decayed. Memories rotted. Beings started becoming Null. This was the beginning of Null Rot.

The protagonist survived, barely, and became furious.

He hunted Null to the ends of the world.

Eventually they returned to the Citadel for the final confrontation. The exact lead-up still needs some detail, but the cleanest reason is that Null announced he would finish the corruption at its source. He did not fully do this the first time because the first corruption was unstable, incomplete, and interrupted during the Citadel defense. This second attempt was meant to be deliberate and final.

The protagonist, already close to death and corruption, threw himself into the disaster while Null tried to absorb/corrupt the power source.

Something went horribly wrong.

The protagonist absorbed part of the power source and part of the corruption. Null and the power source were thrown out of balance.

The protagonist became too powerful.

He killed Null.

This was a catastrophic mistake.

Because Null was not just an enemy. Null was a necessary force of endings. Killing him caused his nullifying power to explode uncontrolled into the deepest parts of the world.

Null Rot spread even further.

## Unbounded

After killing Null, the protagonist became known as **Unbounded**.

He had absorbed part of the power source. He had unimaginable power. He was also partly corrupted, traumatized, and terrified of ever losing again.

This is not "normal dictator villain."

Unbounded is a conflicted protector who becomes a dictator because he believes stability matters more than freedom.

His core belief:

"To keep everything stable, I must keep everything under my control."

But he goes even further than that.

Unbounded freezes progress.

He believes any advancement might eventually create something that could overthrow him, destabilize the world, or allow another Null-level disaster. Even though he is already too powerful, he is still afraid.

So he stagnates the world in the name of safety.

- new dialect research is forbidden
- dangerous algorithms are sealed
- independent repair groups are absorbed or banned
- new tools require Citadel approval
- citizens are stabilized into predictable roles
- historical records are edited
- experiments are labeled corruption risks
- progress is treated as betrayal

Real-life parallels:

- authoritarian regimes using safety to suppress dissent
- leaders who fear innovation because it threatens their control
- institutions that protect stability by killing growth
- trauma responses where someone freezes life to avoid future pain

Unbounded does not think he is evil.

That is the problem.

He thinks he is the only one strong enough to keep the world from collapsing.

### How Unbounded Makes Everything Worse

Unbounded tries to fix the world by giving beings more power.

He thinks if everyone is stronger, nobody will be helpless before Null again.

But power without stable identity corrupts people faster.

Beings start becoming greedy, distorted, monstrous, or obsessed with their own gimmicks.

This gives rise to figures like:

- Bogolord
- algorithmic tyrants
- corrupted sorting nobles
- priority monsters
- beings that turn inputs, data structures, and algorithms into chaos

The idea is that Unbounded unintentionally creates beings whose worst-case behavior can feel like O(unbounded).

Everyone he loves and cares about eventually becomes corrupted by either Null Rot, excessive power, fear, or the stagnant world he created.

By the time he realizes his mistake, the world is already spiritually ruined.

## The Time Reversal

Unbounded makes one last desperate attempt.

He tries to achieve negative Big-O time complexity.

Basically: reverse time itself before everything went wrong.

This works, but not cleanly.

It wipes space complexity.

In story terms, it wipes memory.

The protagonist is sent back or reborn into the second timeline, but his memory is gone.

His talent remains.

That is why the player starts with no past but strong dialect knowledge.

He does not remember what happened, but his instincts are compiled around it.

This is a really important distinction:

He does not have memories.

He has execution scars.

## Second Timeline

This is the timeline the player experiences.

The protagonist wakes up without memory, but with Python/dialect knowledge.

He initially believes Null is the main evil and that he has to protect other beings from Null.

This is partly true, but not the full truth.

Because he has no memory but keeps his talent, he progresses faster than before. He solves things earlier. He avoids some mistakes. He starts looking like someone who has seen the route before.

Over time, he starts uncovering pieces of memory.

The reveal that he becomes Unbounded in the first timeline should happen around 65% through the full story.

Before that, we should foreshadow it through:

- enemies recognizing something in him
- old systems responding to him like an administrator
- Null noticing he is "wrong"
- Unbounded-like dialogue coming out of him during stress
- allies worrying that he is too obsessed with fixing everything
- ruins implying that the savior and tyrant were the same person

## Why Null Changes His Plan In The Second Timeline

This is the big Citadel hinge.

In the first timeline, Null tries to absorb/corrupt the power source.

In the second timeline, Null notices something wrong with the protagonist.

The protagonist has no memory, but moves like someone who already lost this battle.

He predicts patterns he should not know.

He avoids choices his first-timeline self made.

He refuses chances to kill Null.

His dialect skill is too advanced for his lived experience.

His soul/file has rewrite scars from the negative-time attempt.

To Null, this is not just "the protagonist acting weird."

Null is Death. He detects things that should have ended.

The protagonist is an undeleted remainder from a collapsed timeline.

He is proof that an ending was cheated.

So Null changes priorities.

The power source is dangerous, but the protagonist is worse from Null's perspective. The protagonist can retry reality. He can carry optimization across endings. He can keep pushing until death loses.

Null's conclusion:

"The engine is not the anomaly. You are."

So instead of absorbing the power source, Null absorbs the protagonist.

Then he smashes the power source.

This gives him the anomaly and destroys the stabilizer at the same time.

That is how the second timeline breaks.

## Unbounded Null

When Null absorbs the protagonist, he also touches the buried residue of first-timeline Unbounded.

The fear. The control. The power. The corrupted future self that still exists underneath the protagonist's wiped memory.

This creates **Unbounded Null**.

Unbounded Null is pure evil, but not randomly.

He is the worst parts of both forces fused together:

- Null's belief that existence should end
- Unbounded's belief that existence should be controlled
- the protagonist's buried fear that he must fix everything alone

Unbounded Null's worldview is something like:

"Everything will be preserved by being erased."

"Everything will be saved by having no will."

This is the nightmare version of both mercy and protection.

At this point, the game can shift into a much darker "Nightmare" phase. Null Rot becomes stronger. The world becomes more unstable. Monsters become more grotesque and powerful. Old battle rules can be corrupted. Familiar places can become wrong.

## Why Null Can Change In The Finale

Null should not suddenly become nice because the plot needs it.

He changes because, when separated from Unbounded Null, he finally understands what he became.

Inside Unbounded Null, Null is not choosing endings anymore. He is being used as a weapon.

His purpose is twisted into domination by absence.

He sees what pure erasure looks like when stripped of mercy.

It is not peace.

It is cruelty.

The protagonist can confront him with the truth:

"You were never meant to hate existence. You were meant to protect it from endless pain."

Null changes because he realizes he mistook mercy for judgment.

His true role is not to decide that everything should end.

His role is to guard the dignity of endings when endings are truly needed.

That makes him rightful again, not soft.

He can still be severe, ancient, frightening, and necessary.

But he is no longer overstepping.

## Finale Direction

The final conflict should not be solved by becoming more powerful than Unbounded Null.

That would repeat the original mistake.

The final answer should be limits.

The protagonist wins by accepting bounds.

The final battle should probably be about separation, constraints, identity, and release.

Possible final structure:

1. The player weakens Unbounded Null by using bounded repairs instead of raw power.
2. The player separates Null from Unbounded.
3. Null regains his rightful role as Death, not annihilation.
4. Unbounded is revealed as too corrupted to save.
5. Unbounded asks the protagonist to end him.
6. The protagonist must accept that not everything can be repaired.
7. Null performs his true function: release, not murder.

This is where self-sacrifice can come in.

Unbounded, separated from Null, cannot be fixed anymore because he is too corrupted and too entangled with the damaged power source.

He must die to save the world.

The question is whether killing Unbounded also kills the protagonist.

Current possible endings:

### Ending A: The Protagonist Lives, But Loses The Unbounded Path

This is the cleanest option for future expansion.

The protagonist kills/releases Unbounded and survives as a finite person. He loses access to infinite power. He keeps memory, responsibility, and limits.

This ending says: you are not doomed to become your worst future.

### Ending B: The Protagonist Dies Too

This is the tragic version.

The protagonist and Unbounded are too causally linked. Killing Unbounded deletes the loop, and the protagonist sacrifices himself so the world can continue.

This is powerful, but maybe too final unless we really want that.

### Ending C: The Protagonist Becomes The New Bound

This is the mythic version.

The protagonist survives but becomes a guardian of limits. Not a ruler. Not a god. More like a living constraint on the power source so nobody can own it again.

He can still exist in some form, but part of him remains tied to the Citadel.

This might be the strongest compromise.

## Final Moral Resolution

The balanced lesson should NOT be:

- progress bad
- death bad
- power bad
- code bad
- repair bad

That is too simple.

The real lesson should be:

- death is not evil, but despair is
- progress is good, but reckless progress without humility is dangerous
- protection is good, but protection without trust becomes tyranny
- repair is good, but not everything can or should be restored
- power is not automatically corrupting, but fear makes power cling
- uncertainty is not the enemy, but it needs safeguards

The protagonist overcomes his inner struggle by accepting:

1. He cannot save everyone by controlling every outcome.
2. Endings are part of a meaningful world, not just bugs.
3. The future must be allowed to move, even if movement creates risk.
4. A good repair has limits.
5. A person is more than their usefulness.

Possible final exchange:

Null:

"You would permit endings?"

Protagonist:

"I would permit life to be real."

That feels like the heart of it for now.

## Real-Life Parallels To Keep In Mind

Not everything needs a direct real-life parallel. Fiction should still be fiction.

But the story is stronger if some events echo real human patterns:

- people tying self-worth to productivity or usefulness
- overprotective control becoming harmful
- authoritarian safety language used to suppress progress
- institutions freezing innovation because change threatens power
- fear of death turning into denial, obsession, or despair
- grief becoming control
- optimization harming edge cases
- systems treating inconvenient people as bugs
- leaders becoming more afraid the more powerful they get
- progress without ethics creating instability
- refusing to let old things end until they become monstrous

The point is not to lecture.

The point is to make the fantasy feel emotionally true.

## Arcade Mode Direction

Campaign mode can pause for now if needed.

Arcade mode is a very strong direction because it lets players pick enemies and practice patterns without waiting for the full campaign.

Enemy cards can show things like:

- enemy name
- difficulty
- pattern
- prerequisites
- inspired-by LeetCode problems
- battle advantage
- what happens if you do not solve the code mechanic

Example:

Enemy: Hash Wraith

Prerequisites: LeetCode 49, LeetCode 242

Pattern: frequency maps, grouping

Without Code: clones multiply and hide the real body

With Code: matching glyphs are grouped and false clones become vulnerable

This makes the game more LeetCode-like while still being an RPG.

Important: prerequisites should not make the game feel like homework. Better wording might be:

- Inspired by
- Pattern
- Practice links
- Concept lineage

Arcade mode can become the combat lab. Once an enemy is fun there, it can be used in campaign mode.

## Battle Design Direction

Battles should not be "write code, press run, enemy takes damage" forever.

That gets boring fast.

The better model:

1. Enemy presents an unfair battlefield mechanic.
2. Player observes the problem.
3. Player writes or fixes Python code.
4. The code changes the battlefield visibly.
5. Player still has to finish the fight.

Examples:

### Sorting Slime

The player's code controls repair bots that reorder corrupted tiles or slime blobs. You can literally see swaps happen. Better complexity means fewer slime turns.

### Hash Wraith

The player groups glyphs or names. Correct grouping reveals the real body. Wrong grouping creates duplicates or shields.

### Recursive Husk

The enemy contains smaller copies of itself. Correct base cases collapse inner copies. Bad recursion makes the room fold inward.

### Heap Golem

The player writes priority logic. Correct logic repairs urgent hazards first. Bad logic wastes turns on low-priority tiles while the enemy charges.

### Graph Entity

The player writes reachability logic. Correct logic paints safe paths. Bad visited-state handling creates loops, traps, or spreading rot.

## Enemy Difficulty Direction

Some enemies are easy, some are hard, but every enemy should be unfair without the right code advantage.

Easy enemies:

- simple code
- obvious visual feedback
- forgiving damage
- short patterns

Hard enemies:

- harder code
- layered mechanics
- time pressure
- phase changes
- multiple concepts
- worse consequences for inefficient solutions

This creates a better difficulty model:

- Bronze: survive or brute force it
- Silver: solve the main mechanic
- Gold: solve efficiently and take little damage
- S-rank: optimal complexity, clean execution, maybe secret condition

## Tone

The game can be funny, strange, emotional, and unsettling.

It should not be all grimdark.

Bogolord can be ridiculous and still lore-relevant.

Secret rooms can be creepy and almost fourth-wall breaking.

Null can be terrifying without screaming.

Unbounded can be tragic without being excused.

The protagonist can be heroic while still carrying the seed of something dangerous.

That contrast is what makes the world feel alive.

## Inspirations To Keep In Mind

These are inspirations, not characters to copy directly.

Null:

Inspired by Death from *Puss in Boots: The Last Wish*, in the sense that he should feel calm, inevitable, personal, and genuinely frightening without needing to be loud all the time.

The important thing is that Null is Death. He should not feel like a random demon or generic corruption monster. He should feel like something ancient that was always part of the world, and the scary part is that he might even be right about some things before he oversteps.

Unbounded:

Inspired by Baal / the Raiden Shogun from *Genshin Impact*, especially the idea of a ruler trying to preserve stability by freezing progress and enforcing eternity.

Unbounded should have that same kind of tragic authority: someone who is already too powerful, but still afraid of change. He does not stagnate the world because he is stupid. He does it because he believes advancement creates risk, risk creates instability, and instability could bring back disaster.

Overall gameplay feel:

Inspired by *Deltarune*.

Not in the sense of copying its combat directly, but in the sense that Algorithmia can mix RPG exploration, strange enemies, bullet/positioning pressure, choices, humor, unsettling story beats, and battles where the enemy has personality.

The big thing to take from this is that battles should feel alive. The player should not just stare at a code box. The enemy should move, threaten, react, talk, transform, and make the code mechanic feel like part of the fight.

## One-Paragraph Version

Algorithmia is a programming RPG about a memoryless protagonist who wakes up with strange Python-like dialect knowledge and the ability to repair broken reality. He first believes he is fighting Null, an evil force of death spreading Null Rot through the world. The truth is worse: in a previous timeline, the protagonist tried to protect civilization, killed Null, absorbed part of the world's power source, became Unbounded, froze progress in the name of safety, and corrupted the world through fear and excessive power. His final attempt to reverse time wiped his memory but left his talent intact. In the second timeline, Null notices the protagonist is an impossible leftover from a timeline that should have ended, absorbs him instead of the power source, smashes the source, and creates Unbounded Null. The final story is not about becoming stronger than that threat. It is about the protagonist accepting limits, restoring Null as rightful Death instead of annihilation, releasing Unbounded, and learning that repair without bounds becomes control.
